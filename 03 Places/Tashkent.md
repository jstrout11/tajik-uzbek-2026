# Tashkent

Capital of Uzbekistan, ~455m. The modern, Soviet-rebuilt Central Asian capital — flattened by the 1966 earthquake, rebuilt with broad boulevards, brutalist civic monuments, and the **most beautiful metro in Central Asia**. Less postcard than Samarkand, but the city locals actually live in. Our exit point.

`role:: cultural depth + departure`
`days_here:: 2`

## The hit list

### Tashkent Metro — the headline
Opened 1977. **Photography legalized June 2018** after decades of military-installation ban. 48 stations across 4 lines, each themed: Soviet socialist realism meets Islamic design.

Must-see stations (use the metro to get between them — a metro architecture loop is itself the day):
- **Kosmonavtlar** ("Cosmonauts") — space-themed, blue mosaics of Gagarin, Tereshkova, Ulugh Beg, Icarus. Most photogenic.
- **Alisher Navoiy** — Islamic-design, vaulted ceilings, "cyberpunk mosque" aesthetic
- **Paxtakor** — green/blue cotton mosaics
- **Mustaqillik Maydoni** — central, Independence Square
- **Bodomzor** — geometric, less famous, worth a stop

Metro fare: ~1500 UZS (~$0.13) per ride. Single zone. Get a tap card at any station.

> [!tip] Metro photo tips
> Bring a tripod or rest on benches — low light. Wide-angle helps. Guards still occasionally hassle photographers despite the 2018 legalization — be polite, show them the photos, ask "fotografirovat' mozhno?" ("can I photograph?"). Avoid shooting active security checkpoints or guards.

### Khast Imam Complex
Religious center of Tashkent. Houses one of the world's oldest Qurans — the **Uthman Quran**, 8th-century Kufic script, partly preserved (~one-third of the original codex). Plus the **hair of the Prophet Muhammad** relic.

The complex:
- **Moyi Muborak Madrassa** — houses the Uthman Quran in climate-controlled case
- **Barak Khan Madrassa** — courtyard, calligraphy shops
- **Hazrati Imam Mosque** — active mosque, 53m minarets, gold-leaf domes interior
- **Tillashayx Mosque**
- **Imam al-Bukhari Islamic Institute** (working seminary)

Dress conservatively — modest covering, headscarf for her in the active mosque (not required in madrassa-museums). ~90 min minimum.

### Plov Center (Besh Qozon) — lunch institution
- **Address:** by the Tashkent TV Tower. Metro to **Shahriston**, short walk.
- **Hours:** 09:00–23:00 daily — but **plov is lunch only.** Get there before 12:00 to see the cooking (giant kazans, theatrical), eat at 12:30.
- **Multiple locations** — main one is by TV Tower. Other Besh Qozon outlets exist but main is the experience.
- 7,000 meals/day, 5 open-flame kazans
- Tashkent plov: heavier, more meat, **quail egg + horse sausage variants** available (kazi)
- Cost per plate: [plov_center_usd:: 5]
- Pair with non bread and salat (tomato/cucumber/onion). Beer if you want (Sarbast or Pulsar).

### Chorsu Bazaar
**Under the giant blue dome.** Working bazaar, multi-story. Spices, dried fruit, non, fresh meat (gore-warning lower levels), kitchenware. Best in the morning. Adjacent to Kukeldash Madrassa and the Friday Mosque, all clustered.

### Soviet-era brutalism walking route
The 1966 earthquake leveled old Tashkent; the rebuild was peak Soviet civic architecture. Worth a half-day walk:
- **Hotel Uzbekistan** — concrete grid facade, iconic
- **Palace of Forums** + **Independence Square** — fountain-and-monument geometry
- **State Museum of History** (Lenin Museum) — building itself is the attraction, contents secondary
- **Earthquake Memorial** — bronze monument to the 1966 quake
- Walk Amir Timur Avenue south for the residential brutalist stretch

### Amir Timur Square + Museum
Central square with Tamerlane statue and Amir Timur Museum (carpet-tiled drum, Timurid history). Skip the museum unless you want context on the cities you just saw.

## Things to skip
- **Magic City** — themed amusement park, recent, polished tourist trap
- Most "ethnographic" shows in hotel restaurants

## Where to eat

### Plov
- **Plov Center / Besh Qozon** — see above. Lunch.

### Other meals
- **Bibikhanum** (also written Bibikhonim) — Uzbek classics in elegant setting, central, mid-range
- **Caravan** — chic Uzbek-modern fusion, near Amir Timur Square
- **Affresco** — Italian if you've had enough plov
- **Saray** restaurant chain — reliable Uzbek, English menu, multiple locations

### Bars + nightlife (if energy)
- **PUB Cervecería** or any of the Sarbast / Pulsar beer halls — Russian-style beer halls
- **Brew Pub** at Hyatt — craft scene, expensive
- Wine: nothing yet, head to Samarkand for the wine scene

## Where to sleep

Tashkent's hotel scene is broader than Samarkand — Soviet-era classics, modern business hotels, recent boutiques. Stay central (Mirabad / Yunusabad / near Amir Timur Square).

- **Hyatt Regency Tashkent** — international 5-star, ~$200, splurge
- **Hotel Uzbekistan** — Soviet-era icon, recently renovated, ~$80–120, central location, **stay for the architecture experience**
- **Boutique Art Hotel Khiva** — small, character, ~$80
- **City Palace Hotel** — mid-range, central, ~$60–80
- **Topchan Hostel** — budget, well-rated, near center, $20–30 twin

## Logistics

### Arrival from Samarkand (Day 12)
- **Afrosiyob train** ~2h 10m, ~$25 economy
- Arrives Tashkent Yuzhniy (Southern) station
- Taxi to hotel ~10–15 min, ~30,000 UZS via Yandex Go

### Departure (Day 18, 09:35)
- Leave hotel **06:00 latest**
- TAS airport ~12 km from center, 20 min off-peak
- Yandex Go pre-book or hotel taxi
- International check-in opens 3h before flight — be at counter by 06:30

### Getting around
- **Metro** is fastest + cheapest + the most interesting tour
- **Yandex Go** for door-to-door
- City taxis on street: avoid (no meters, gouging)
- Walk: central old-town streets are walkable; brutalist axis is too spread out

## Two-traveler logistics
- **Mosque dress at Khast Imam active mosque** — headcovering for her (bring scarf, it stays in her daypack rolled up)
- **Plov Center seating** — can be communal long tables, fine for two
- **Metro security at major stations** — bag checks (X-ray), no liquids hassle. Smooth.
