# Health

## Altitude (primary concern)

### Profile of this trip
| Location | Elevation | Day |
|---|---:|---|
| Dushanbe | 800m | 1–2 |
| Anzob Pass (over road) | 3372m | 3 (brief, in vehicle) |
| Penjikent | 1000m | 3, 9 |
| [[Artush and Pasrud]] trailhead | ~2200m | 4 |
| Kulikalon Lakes camp | ~2800m | 5 |
| Chukurak Pass (over) | ~3180m | 5 |
| Alaudin Pass (over) | **3860m** | 7 |
| Alaudin Lakes camp | ~2800m | 7 |

### Acclimatization plan
- Day 1–2 Dushanbe (800m): no concern
- **Day 3 Anzob Pass crossing (3372m road)** = first altitude exposure, ~30 min in vehicle. Watch for AMS symptoms in the afternoon. Sleep low (Penjikent 1000m).
- Day 4 trek up to ~2500m camp: easy
- Day 5 Chukurak Pass (3180m) crossing + Kulikalon camp (2800m): moderate. Watch for AMS that evening.
- **Day 7 Alaudin Pass (3860m)** = high point. We arrive having slept 3 nights between 2500–2800m. Reasonable acclimatization profile.

> [!tip] Diamox protocol
> Start **prophylactic Diamox 125mg 2× daily** the morning before the Anzob Pass crossing (Day 3). Continue through Day 8 (post-Alaudin). Side effects: tingling extremities, weird taste in carbonated drinks, mild diuresis. Discuss with travel medicine doc.

### AMS symptoms — when to descend
- Headache + nausea/vomiting + ataxia = **HACE risk**, descend immediately
- Pink frothy sputum + extreme breathlessness at rest = **HAPE risk**, descend immediately
- Mild headache and reduced appetite alone = acclimatization; rest, hydrate, ibuprofen
- The mountaineer has been higher (Chimborazo 6263m, Cotopaxi 5897m) — knows the symptoms. Cross-check with partner who may not.

## Water
- **Cities (DYU, Penjikent, Samarkand, Tashkent):** do not drink tap. Bottled water everywhere, ~3–8000 UZS / 5 TJS per 1.5L.
- **Trek:** filter (Sawyer Squeeze) or iodine all water. Streams run clear but livestock graze upstream.
- **Restaurants:** ask for "ne gazirovannaya voda" (still water) bottled, or stick to tea (boiled).
- Avoid raw salads/vegetables outside higher-end hotels; cooked is safe.

## Vaccinations
Visit travel clinic **6–8 weeks before flight** for routine boosters and:

- [ ] **Hepatitis A** — strongly recommended
- [ ] **Typhoid** — recommended (food/water exposure)
- [ ] Tetanus booster if >10 years
- [ ] MMR up to date
- [ ] Hepatitis B (consider — booster if previously vaccinated)
- [ ] Rabies (consider — only if planning extended remote village stays; not strictly needed for our profile)
- [ ] COVID booster current

Not generally recommended for this trip: yellow fever, Japanese encephalitis, cholera.

## Medical kit (split between two travelers)

### Drugs
- Diamox 125mg (acetazolamide) — altitude prophylaxis
- Dexamethasone — emergency descent meds (script from doctor, only for HACE response while descending)
- Ibuprofen 400mg, paracetamol 500mg
- Cipro or azithromycin — GI infection (doctor's choice)
- Loperamide (Imodium) — symptomatic GI
- Antiemetic (ondansetron) — vomiting / motion sickness
- Antihistamine (loratadine/cetirizine)
- Hydrocortisone 1% cream

### Field
- Blister kit (Compeed/Leukotape)
- Athletic tape, kinesio tape
- Steri-strips, gauze, bandage rolls
- Tweezers, scissors, safety pins
- Tincture of iodine / Betadine (skin disinfection)
- SAM splint
- Electrolyte tabs/powder (LMNT, Nuun)
- Sunscreen SPF 50+ (multiple tubes), zinc stick, SPF lip balm

### Personal
- Any prescription meds in original bottles (TSA + customs)
- Glasses + contacts backup
- **Tampons / pads — bring full trip supply.** Limited availability in TJ, especially outside Dushanbe. UZ better but inconsistent brands.

## Pharmacies on the ground
- Dushanbe: well-stocked, "apteka" sign, central pharmacies have English-speaking staff sometimes
- Penjikent: small pharmacy, basics only — don't rely on it for anything you need
- Trek: nothing — bring everything in
- Samarkand / Tashkent: well-stocked, more brand-name availability

## Insurance

> [!warning] Confirm altitude coverage explicitly
> Standard travel insurance often excludes trekking above 3000m or 4500m depending on policy. Alaudin Pass at 3860m is in the gap. Read fine print. Options that cover:
> - **World Nomads Explorer** — typically covers to 6000m
> - **IMG Patriot / GeoBlue** — solid for remote evac
> - **Global Rescue** membership — separate from insurance, covers helicopter evacuation specifically

### Action
- [ ] Buy travel insurance, confirm trekking-to-4000m + remote evac coverage
- [ ] Save policy + 24h emergency phone number offline on phone
- [ ] Brief partner on insurance contact procedure if mountaineer is the incapacitated one

## On-the-ground

### Food safety
- Hot, fresh, cooked: safe everywhere
- Plov from a busy choyxona at lunch: safe (high turnover)
- Plov at dinner sitting since noon: skip
- Raw veg, salads outside reputable restaurants: skip
- Yogurt (qurutob, kefir) at homes/homestays: generally safe (fermented)
- Tap water: never

### Sun
UV at 3000m is roughly 30% stronger than sea level. Snow/lake reflection multiplies it. SPF 50, glacier sunglasses, brimmed hat, lip balm — non-negotiable on trek.

### Cold injuries
Frostbite risk on Alaudin Pass (3860m) if windbound or snowing. Standard prevention: mittens > gloves at speed, beanie/balaclava on, don't sit on rocks, eat frequently.

### GI
Even with precautions, expect 1–2 days of mild GI. The mountaineer can probably push through; partner may need a rest day. Build into trek schedule (we have Day 9 buffer post-trek).
