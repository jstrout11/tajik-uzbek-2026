# Food & Culture

## Plov — the regional fight

**Tashkent style** — heavier, more meat. Quail egg and horse sausage (kazi) add-ons. Mixed in serving. Eat at **Plov Center / Besh Qozon** — see [[Tashkent]].

**Samarkand style** — lighter, **layered** (rice base, meat-and-carrot stew on top), presented unmixed. Sweeter from raisins. Eat at lunch — see [[Samarkand]]. Karimbek and the choyxonas near Siyob Bazaar are reliable.

**Bukhara style** — sweetest, more raisins and dried apricots. Not visiting this trip, parked for next time.

> [!tip] Plov is lunch
> Plov sells out 12:00–14:00 because it's cooked once a day in giant kazans and not refrigerated. After 14:00 it's been sitting; by dinner, **skip**. Anywhere advertising "plov dinner special" is a tourist trap or a microwave.

## UZ staples

### Lagman
Hand-pulled noodles in broth or fried. Uyghur origin — best at Uyghur-run places. Most "lagman" at generic restaurants is decent but lacks the chewy hand-pull texture. Look for places with a flour-dusted noodle station visible.

### Shashlik
Skewers — mutton, beef, sometimes chicken or fish (rare). Best is **kazan kebab** style (slow-cooked) at choyxonas. Roadside grills are usually fine if the fire is going.

### Samsa
Tandoor-baked savory pastries. **Beef**, **pumpkin (kovok)**, or **lamb-and-onion**. Triangular shape. 5–10k UZS each at bazaars. Best from a working tandoor oven — pumpkin samsa in particular is a Samarkand specialty.

### Non bread
- Round flatbread, stamped pattern (each city has its own stamp design)
- **Don't put non upside down on the table** — bad luck, also disrespectful to the bread
- **Don't drop non** — a guest is supposed to break, not slice
- Tear into pieces, dip in stew or eat with hands

### Tea
- **Green tea (kok choy)** in UZ — default, comes in a porcelain pot (choynak) with small bowls (piala)
- **Both green and black** common in TJ
- The "three-pour ritual": pour from pot into piala, pour back into pot, repeat 3× before serving — aerates and steeps consistently
- **Half-full piala = welcome, stay longer** (host has to keep refilling, which extends conversation)
- **Full piala = polite "you're done"** (host's signal to wrap)

## TJ staples

### Qurutob — the national dish
- Flatbread torn into a wooden bowl (tabaq)
- **Qurut** (dried fermented yogurt) reconstituted with water + oil + onion + herbs poured over
- Sometimes tomato, cucumber, fried meat on top
- **Eaten by hand from a communal platter** — sit cross-legged around the tabaq
- Best at family-run choyxonas in Dushanbe. Ask locals.

### Shurbo
Mutton-and-vegetable soup. Slow-cooked. Different from Uzbek versions in being more dairy-heavy (yogurt sometimes added). Served as starter with non bread.

### Manti
Large steamed dumplings — lamb + onion + tail fat. Common across the region but more associated with TJ menus. Eaten with sour cream or yogurt.

### Sambusa / Samsa
Same idea as UZ samsa but baked in more variety in TJ. Look for **goosht sambusa** (meat).

## Choyxona etiquette
The choyxona (teahouse) is the social center of any town. Etiquette:

- **Shoes off on the topchan** (raised platform with cushions). Look for a shoe pile near the entrance.
- **Sit cross-legged or side-saddle** on the topchan. Don't sit with legs stretched out toward food or older people.
- **Don't point feet at others or the food** — feet are dirty/low-status in Islamic etiquette
- **Bread first** — non always comes first, before any other food
- **Pay at the end** — don't tip the tea
- **Smoking** — varies; ask. Many choyxonas tolerate it on the topchan.

## Religious site etiquette

### Mosques (active)
- **Shoulders and knees covered**, both of us
- **Headscarf for her** in active mosque (carry packable scarf in daypack)
- **Shoes off** at entrance
- **No photos during prayer**
- The active mosque inside Khast Imam complex in Tashkent is the main "real" mosque we visit; the rest are museum-only

### Madrassas (most are museum-only)
- **Shoulders and knees still covered** (modesty norm, not religious)
- **Shoes off** at entrance
- **No headscarf needed** (these are heritage sites, not active mosques)
- Photos fine
- Examples: Registan madrassas (Samarkand), Khast Imam madrassas (Tashkent except the active mosque)

### Mausoleums
- Quiet voices
- Shoulders + knees covered
- Shoes off if there's a shoe pile
- Photos generally fine, no flash near tiled surfaces

## Photographing people

- **Always ask.** "Mozhno?" ("may I?") works in Russian. Smile + camera-gesture works universally.
- **Bazaar vendors** usually say yes and want to see the photo on your screen
- **Religious leaders / imams** — defer to their preference
- **Don't photograph:** military, police, borders, security checkpoints, anyone clearly uncomfortable
- **Tashkent metro photography is legal** since June 2018 but guards may not have the memo — keep a phone ready to show them the "no restriction" articles if hassled, or just move along

## Ramadan in October 2026
**Ramadan 2026 falls roughly Feb 17 – Mar 19.** Our trip is well clear. No fasting considerations.

## Alcohol

### Tajikistan
- Vodka heavy (Soviet legacy). Will be offered at hospitality moments. Refusing is fine but acknowledge the toast.
- Local beer: Sim-Sim, Sayyod — drinkable
- Wine: nothing notable
- **Trek note:** horsemen may share vodka at camp; one round is hospitality, three rounds is a problem at altitude

### Uzbekistan
- Beer: **Sarbast** (most common), **Pulsar** (also good). Lager-style, drinkable.
- Wine: nascent scene — **Samarkand Wine Center** does tastings. Uzbek wine is improving, lean dry whites are best bets.
- Vodka still common but less default than TJ

## Bargaining
- Bazaar prices have a tourist markup of 30–50%
- Offer 50–60% of asking, settle around 70%
- "Dorogo" ("expensive") + a sad face moves prices fast
- Smile, take your time, walk away once — they'll often call you back with a better number
- **Don't bargain on food prices** (street, bazaar food stalls) — those are flat-priced
- Bargaining is OK on textiles, ceramics, "antiques," carpets, taxis (negotiate before getting in)

## Tipping
- **Restaurants:** 5–10%. Often "service" already on bill. Check.
- **Choyxonas:** rare; round up if you want
- **Drivers (multi-day):** ~10% on top
- **Horsemen / guides:** ~10% of fee at end, plus a small gift if you have one (Snickers, headlamp, multi-tool)
- **Hotel housekeeping:** $1–2 per night if you stay multiple nights and they did real work

## Greetings + interactions

- **Assalomu alaykum** — works everywhere, all ages. Reply: "Wa alaykum assalom."
- Handshake **with right hand** (left is for ablutions, considered unclean)
- Place left hand on heart while shaking — sign of warmth
- **Don't extend handshake to a woman first if you're a man** — let her extend if she chooses. (Both directions, both directions of gender, observe local cues.)
- Elders first in all things — greetings, seats, food
