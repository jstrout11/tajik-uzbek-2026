# History — what you're looking at and why

A working knowledge of the region's history makes the trip 5× more interesting. Most monuments you'll see lock onto specific eras below. The Samarkand–Bukhara monumental scale is from one ~50-year window. The Tashkent metro is from another. Both make more sense once you can place them.

## 1. Sogdiana (3rd–8th c. CE) — the Silk Road's middlemen
The original Silk Road merchant civilization. Eastern Iranian people, network of oasis cities — **Samarkand, Bukhara, Penjikent**. Not a centralized empire, but a confederation of city-states. They ran the trade routes between China and Byzantium for 500 years.

- **Languages:** Sogdian (Eastern Iranian, ancestor of modern Tajik via Persian family)
- **Religion:** Zoroastrian and Manichaean primarily; Buddhist, Nestorian Christian, Jewish minorities
- **Art:** the frescoes you'll see at [[Penjikent]] — palace murals, hunting scenes, courtly dancers
- **Why this matters at sites:** Penjikent (the ancient city, not the modern town) is the **best-preserved Sogdian city anywhere**. Abandoned in 722 CE after the Arab conquest, never reoccupied — fossilized in time. The frescoes are split between Penjikent's museum, Dushanbe's National Museum of Antiquities (the originals), and the Hermitage in St. Petersburg.

## 2. Arab conquest & Samanid renaissance (8th–10th c.)
**Qutayba ibn Muslim** brought the Umayyad Caliphate east in 706–715 CE. Penjikent fell in 722 — abandoned, not razed. Bukhara taken 709; Samarkand 712. Conversion to Islam was gradual, taking 200+ years.

The **Samanid dynasty** (819–999) was Central Asia's first post-Arab Persian-Tajik state. **Bukhara was its capital** — a center of Persian-Islamic literature, science, medicine (Avicenna / Ibn Sina was Bukharan). The brick **Samanid Mausoleum** still stands in Bukhara — the oldest extant Islamic monument in Central Asia.

- **Why this matters to Tajik identity:** Modern Tajikistan's national hero **Ismoil Somoni** (Ismail Samani) — namesake of the currency, the Dushanbe statue, the highest peak — is the founder of this dynasty. Tajiks consider this period their cultural origin point.
- **Not visiting Bukhara this trip,** but parked for next.

## 3. Mongols → Timurids (13th–15th c.) — the monumental era
**Genghis Khan** rolled through in 1220. Bukhara and Samarkand were destroyed. The Mongol invasion is the great civilizational rupture — populations slaughtered, libraries burned, cities depopulated for generations.

The rebuild came under **Tamerlane (Timur, r. 1370–1405)** — Turco-Mongol conqueror, born in **Shahrisabz** near Samarkand. He made **Samarkand his capital** and conscripted artisans from every city he conquered — Persia, India, Anatolia — to build it back as the wonder of the world.

**This is the architectural era you'll see at sites:**
- **Gur-e-Amir** — Tamerlane's tomb (you'll visit)
- **Bibi-Khanym Mosque** — built for his wife
- **Shah-i-Zinda** — Timurid mausoleums grafted onto an older pilgrimage site
- **Registan** — actually slightly later (15th–17th c., grandson Ulugh Beg's madrassa is the original)
- **Ak-Saray Palace ruins** in Shahrisabz (skipped this trip)
- The turquoise-and-cobalt tile aesthetic, the iwan portals, the bulbous melon-ribbed domes — all Timurid signatures

**Ulugh Beg** (Tamerlane's grandson, r. 1447–49) — astronomer-king, built the **Ulugh Beg Observatory** at Samarkand (you'll visit). Calculated the length of the year to within a minute. Murdered by his own son.

## 4. Khanates (16th–19th c.) — the slow decline
Post-Timurid Central Asia fragmented into three Uzbek khanates: **Bukhara**, **Khiva**, **Kokand**. Silk Road declined as maritime trade routes took over. The cities you'll see were already 200-year-old monuments by the time the Russians showed up.

## 5. Russian conquest (1865–1885) → Soviet era (1924–1991)
**Tashkent fell to the Russians in 1865**, Samarkand 1868, Bukhara became a protectorate. **Russian Turkestan** was the colonial governorship until the 1917 revolution.

### The 1924 border draw — Stalin and the Samarkand paradox
After the Soviets consolidated power, in **1924 they redrew Central Asia along "national" lines** under Lenin's Commissar for Nationalities — Joseph Stalin. The five Soviet Central Asian republics were carved out: Uzbek, Tajik, Turkmen, Kazakh, Kyrgyz.

**The Samarkand–Bukhara problem:** Both cities have historically been **majority Tajik-speaking** (Persian-family), culturally Tajik centers. But they were drawn into the **Uzbek SSR**.

Why? Several reasons cited by historians:
- The Turkic-speaking (Uzbek) countryside surrounded both cities — contiguous-territory logic
- Economic-viability requirements (each republic needed minimum population, rail access)
- Possibly: deliberate weakening of Tajik national identity ("divide and rule" theory, though contested — see Eurasianet "Stalin's Giant Pencil")
- Possibly: many residents identified as **Sart** (urban, Persian-Turkic mixed) rather than firmly Tajik or Uzbek

**Result:** Modern Tajikistan exists without its two historical cultural capitals. This is still a sensitive subject — when you're in Samarkand, you'll hear Tajik spoken in the bazaars even though it's officially Uzbekistan.

### Soviet legacy you'll see
- **Russian language everywhere** — still the lingua franca for anyone 30+ in both countries
- **Tashkent rebuilt brutalist** — the **1966 earthquake** flattened the old city. Soviet planners rebuilt with concrete, wide boulevards, civic monuments. Brutalism walk in [[Tashkent]] is the result.
- **Tashkent Metro (1977)** — Central Asia's first, designed as a nuclear bunker. Photography illegal until 2018.
- **Dushanbe** is essentially a Soviet creation — small village in 1929, declared Tajik SSR capital, built from scratch
- **Alplagers (mountaineering basecamps)** — Soviet mass-mountaineering infrastructure. Artuch Alplager (where we start the trek) is one.

## 6. Independence (1991) to now

### Tajikistan: civil war and recovery
- Independence Sept 1991. Civil war erupted **1992–1997** — regional/clan factions, ~100,000 dead.
- President **Emomali Rahmon** in power since 1994. Authoritarian, dynastic. "Stability over openness."
- Economy depends heavily on remittances from Tajik workers in Russia.
- Tourism opening cautiously over the past decade — e-visa system, GBAO permits accessible.

### Uzbekistan: from closed to open
- Independence 1991. President **Islam Karimov** ruled 1991–2016 — closed, repressive, hard for tourists to visit.
- Karimov died 2016. Successor **Shavkat Mirziyoyev** has substantially opened the country: visa-free for many nationalities, currency reforms (the bazaar moneychanger thing), tourism push.
- The fact that we can fly in visa-free in 2026 is a direct result of the post-2016 reforms.
- Bazaars open, English on signs in Samarkand/Bukhara, train booking online — all post-2016.

## Books worth reading before you go
- **The Lost Heart of Asia**, Colin Thubron — travelogue post-Soviet 1990s, beautifully written, sets the mood
- **Shadow of the Silk Road**, Thubron — broader Silk Road context
- **The Great Game**, Peter Hopkirk — Russian/British imperial rivalry, fun
- **Empires of the Silk Road**, Christopher Beckwith — academic but the definitive
- **Murder in Samarkand**, Craig Murray — Karimov-era Uzbekistan from a British ambassador's view
